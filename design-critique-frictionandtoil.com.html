Critical · Priority 1

Turnaround claim contradicts itself

Hero reads "ready within 48 hours". FAQ answers "Five working days from briefing to delivery." Same page, same product, two different promises. For a £500 credibility product this is the single highest-risk line on the page.

Critical · Priority 2

Every CTA is a Cloudflare-obfuscated mailto

"Order your report — £500" resolves to /cdn-cgi/l/email-protection#… which JavaScript decodes to a mailto. A visitor ready to buy has to compose an email. No form, no Stripe link, no Calendly. Highest-friction possible checkout.

Medium · Priority 3

Brand mismatch with homepage

Homepage is editorial-luxury (Outfit 200 + Cormorant italic, cinematic bridge). This page is modern-SaaS (Space Grotesk + Inter, indigo/amber/emerald). Same company, two different design languages. Pick one identity.

Executive summary

This is a well-built direct-response landing page. The nine-section arc (hero → pain → cost → solution → 8 specialist areas → 5 dimensions → pricing comparison → sample report → testimonials → FAQ) is textbook, and it's executed with real craft: animated 5-ring hero visualisation, semantic colour system per dimension, Schema.org Product + FAQPage + Organization markup, and disciplined typography scale.

What holds it back from a 90+ isn't design execution — it's two tactical breaks in the buyer's trust arc: a self-contradicting turnaround promise, and a checkout path that forces a manual email at the moment of commitment. Fix those two and this page is ready. The brand-identity divergence from the homepage is a quieter issue but worth resolving before scaling marketing spend.

Current pattern

Direct-response SaaS landing page, long-form

Sticky nav → Hero (copy + animated 5-ring rating) → The Problem (4-card impact stack) → What it's costing you (5 flip cards) → The Solution → The Intelligence (8 specialist areas) → 5 Dimensions → Why £500 (3-card comparison) → Sample report (chip row + "open full report" CTA) → Testimonials → FAQ → Footer.

This is a strong pattern for a mid-ticket information product. It mirrors the framework's SaaS reference (Hero + Features + Social Proof + CTA) and adds three things it should add: pain agitation, pricing rationale, and anchored proof (the wedding-venue sample report).

Information architecture — what works

• Hero pairs an emotional H1 with a visible rating system — the visitor sees the product before they read about it.
• "Why £500" section places F&T between the £3–8k agency audit and the £1,188/yr DIY tool. Clean price-anchoring.
• Sample report card is genuinely useful — visitors can click through to a real (anonymised) deliverable. Most competitors gate this.
• FAQ carries Schema.org markup → eligible for Google's FAQ rich results.

IA — where it leaks

• Only one CTA action exists (email). There's no secondary path for a visitor who's curious but not ready — no "get a one-page summary", no calendar link, no newsletter.
• The 8 specialist areas and the 5 dimensions sit back-to-back and partially overlap in meaning. A visitor may read them as the same thing said twice. Consider collapsing, or explicitly framing 8-areas as "how we do it" and 5-dimensions as "what you get".
• Testimonials section has only 2 cards — on a page selling trust, three feels minimum. Four is better.

Aesthetic read

Dark modern SaaS with semantic colour system

The page lives in the Linear/Vercel aesthetic neighbourhood — dark background, subtle translucent cards (rgba(255,255,255,0.03)), hairline borders, soft hover lifts. The 5-dimension colour system (indigo / amber / emerald / rose / cyan) gives it a unique fingerprint — you're not just using one accent, you're using a palette-as-taxonomy. This is confident and rare.

The animated 5-ring hero is the signature asset. It's a compact visual argument — five dimensions, each with a visible score, assembled around a single "Overall: 54/100". It sells the product by being the product. Strong.

Where it misaligns with the brand

The homepage sells "Strategic Intelligence" in an editorial, cinematic register — thin Outfit wordmarks, Cormorant italic, atmospheric SVG bridge, near-silent dark. This page sells the same brand in a direct-response register — bold Space Grotesk H1, multi-colour system, pain-focused copy ("Your website is losing you money right now").

Both are good pages. But a visitor who journeys homepage → web-intelligence experiences tonal whiplash. Decide: is Friction & Toil an editorial intelligence house (quiet, confident, senior) or a direct-response martech product (pain, urgency, proof)? Then harmonise.

Premium details worth preserving

• Hairline rgba(255,255,255,0.08) borders sit well under the dim background without competing.
• Sticky header uses backdrop-filter:blur(14px) with an 0.78 opacity base — classic glass, tastefully applied.
• Pricing comparison's "is-featured" card uses a subtle scale/translate to lift F&T above the alternatives — the anchoring is visual as well as verbal.

The 5 dimension colours are used semantically — not decoratively. That's the correct discipline and it's rare. Each dimension owns its colour in the rings, chips, dim-cards, and legend. This creates instant visual learning: after one scroll, a visitor knows amber = design without being told.

What to refine

1. No singular brand accent. The five semantic colours are great for reports, but the brand itself needs one anchor colour. Currently, the badge pill tick glows amber (design) — by accident or design, amber is doing double duty as brand accent. Either commit to that (amber = F&T) or introduce a 6th, brand-only colour (e.g. bone white, brass).
2. CTA button is white (.btn-white). Functional but unmemorable. Painting it amber would both reinforce brand and match the hero badge tick — one colour move, two wins.
3. Muted text #8B8B9E on #0A0A0F at 15–17px passes AA. At 12px eyebrow sizes the #5A5A6E muted-dim is borderline — check the "Why £500" context line.

Typography — current

Three families, role-separated:
• Space Grotesk 500/600 — H1, section headings, display numbers. Geometric, slightly quirky "a", confident.
• Inter 400/500 — body, UI, eyebrows, nav. Neutral workhorse.
• JetBrains Mono 400/500 — scores, stats, data chips. Implicit "this is measured intelligence, not marketing fluff."

The role-separation is excellent: display for persuasion, sans for reading, mono for proof. Scale clamps (clamp(2rem,4.5vw,3.25rem) on section heads) handle responsive well.

Typography — to fix

• Brand pair mismatch with homepage. The homepage uses Outfit + Cormorant. If both pages are meant to co-exist, either the homepage should pick up Space Grotesk in its nav, or this page should carry one editorial touch (Cormorant italic & somewhere — in "Friction & Toil" footer wordmark, or as a pull-quote accent in testimonials).
• Eyebrow at 0.72rem with 0.3em tracking is on the tight side — that's roughly 11.5px with heavy spacing. At small viewports this risks visual jitter. Bump to 0.75rem minimum.
• H1 line-break <br> forces the split "Your website is losing / you money right now." — works on desktop, awkward on mid-width. Remove the hard break and let a max-width constraint handle wrap.

Animation audit

Three animation systems detected:
1. Staggered reveal — .reveal with d1–d5 delay classes, IntersectionObserver-style entry on scroll. Standard and clean.
2. Ring dashoffset animation — the 5 hero arcs animate from stroke-dashoffset: circumference → (circumference × (100 − score)/100) on viewport entry. Score counter (0 → 54) counts up in parallel.
3. Card hover lifts — translate and border-colour transitions on impact cards, flip cards, and pricing cards.

All three are on-spec for the framework: transform/opacity only, sub-600ms, staggered at ~100ms. No blocking animations. No autoplay video. Good discipline.

Missing

No prefers-reduced-motion media query. Same miss as the homepage. For a product whose core asset is an animated visualisation, this is the most important accessibility fix on the page:

@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) {
  .reveal, .ring-arc, .ring-center-score {
    animation: none !important;
    transition: none !important;
    opacity: 1 !important;
    transform: none !important;
    stroke-dashoffset: var(--final-offset) !important;
  }
}

Inline onmouseover/onmouseout on the shared nav. Same carry-over from the homepage nav. Replace with CSS :hover.

UX micro-issues

• The CTA .btn-white relies on Cloudflare's email-decode script (email-decode.min.js). With JavaScript disabled or blocked, the "Order your report — £500" button resolves to a garbled URL. The whole purchase path is JS-dependent for no structural reason. A visible mailto:hello@frictionandtoil.com would be better even with the spam risk.
• FAQ <details> elements don't animate open — they jump. A 200ms max-height transition would align them with the rest of the page's motion grammar.
• No focus-visible styles detected on nav links or buttons. Keyboard users get the default browser ring, which on a dark theme can be invisible.

Conflicting turnaround promise: 48 hours vs 5 working days
Critical

Hero sub: "Tell us your URL. We'll have your report ready within 48 hours." FAQ answer: "Five working days from briefing to delivery." Schema.org FAQPage markup means the 5-day answer could appear directly in Google results even as the hero advertises 48 hours.

Fix: pick one. If 5 days is the real SLA, change the hero to "Your report delivered within five working days" and update the FAQPage JSON-LD to match. If you can truly hit 48 hours, change the FAQ (and make it the headline promise — it's a competitive weapon).

No real checkout — every CTA opens a mailto
Critical

Every "Order your report — £500" button routes to a Cloudflare-obfuscated mailto: with pre-filled subject and body. A buyer who has read the full page and is ready to pay must: (a) have a mail client configured, (b) compose, (c) wait for a human reply, (d) wait for an invoice, (e) pay. That's five steps between "yes" and "paid."

Fix: add Stripe Payment Link (single-use, £500, GBP). Keep the mailto as a secondary "questions?" link. On submit of payment, redirect to a "brief us" form that captures URL + sector + goals.

Two dead nav links (#)
Critical

Shared header on this page and the homepage. Strategic Intelligence and Experiments both point to #. Clicking them adds # to the URL and nothing happens. On a page selling professional intelligence at £500, broken navigation reads as "not quite ready yet."

Fix: stub routes — even a "Coming soon — join the waitlist" page — or remove those links from the nav until pages exist.

Brand-identity fork from homepage
High

Homepage: Outfit 200 + Cormorant italic, monochrome, cinematic SVG. This page: Space Grotesk + Inter + JBM, five-colour semantic palette, direct-response copy. The shared 56px sticky nav is the only thing binding them visually.

Fix: introduce one shared anchor asset — e.g. the editorial Friction & Toil wordmark (Outfit 200 + Cormorant italic ampersand) must appear on both pages, and one colour or motif from the homepage bridge (the brass/amber highlight) should carry into the Web Intelligence CTA.

Only two testimonials
High

Two quotes is below the "trust floor" for a £500 mid-ticket product. Also — both signatories are plausible but generic ("Independent Hotel Group", "Professional Services Firm"). No company name, no linkedin link, no photo.

Fix: add 2 more testimonials. For each, use a real company name and a headshot (with permission). If you can't get four real ones yet, show one and replace the rest with a case-study card that links to the sample report.

No prefers-reduced-motion guard
High

Reveal animations and ring-arc animations run unconditionally. Accessibility failure, same as homepage.

Fix: add the media query from the Motion tab. The ring should render at its final offset for reduced-motion users; the counter should render at its final value.

Two overlapping "features" sections
Medium

"The Intelligence — eight specialist areas" and "Five dimensions — one report" are consecutive and semantically adjacent. A visitor may read them as redundancy.

Fix: reframe. 8 areas = "Our process" (how we do it). 5 dimensions = "Your report" (what you get). Or collapse 8→5 and retire the redundant section.

CTA button is plain white
Medium

.btn-white is the primary action. Functional, contrasts well, but invisible as brand — any page on the internet could have that button.

Fix: paint it amber (#C9963A) to match the hero badge tick. That's already your brand's ownable accent — use it.

H1 hard line break
Medium

<h1>Your website is losing<br>you money right now.</h1> — forces an awkward wrap at mid-viewport widths.

Fix: remove <br>, add max-width: 18ch to the H1.

Inline handlers on shared nav
Medium

Same inline onmouseover/onmouseout colour swaps as homepage. CSP-unfriendly, DRY-unfriendly.

Fix: extract to a shared stylesheet. Both pages inherit cleanly.

Reconcile the turnaround promise across hero + FAQ + Schema.org JSON-LD. Stub or remove dead nav links. Add reduced-motion guard. Swap CTA to amber. These five changes can ship in one commit and collectively lift the page by 6–8 points.

Add a Stripe Payment Link (£500 GBP). On success → redirect to a simple Typeform/Tally brief (URL, sector, primary competitor, goals). Keep mailto as a "questions?" fallback. This is the single highest-revenue change on the roadmap.

Week 3 — Brand harmonisation

Introduce one shared asset across homepage and this page: the editorial Outfit 200 + Cormorant italic Friction & Toil wordmark. Use amber #C9963A as the single CTA colour on both pages. Keep Space Grotesk for section heads on long-form pages, keep editorial for brand/wordmark.

Week 4 — Proof pass
